About

This project draws from the work of Walk Free, WikiRate, and the Business & Human Rights Resource Centre, to assess the statements produced by 79 out of 91 asset managers identified as having to report under the UK Modern Slavery Act. We wanted to understand the level of awareness of modern slavery risks among asset managers and to highlight gaps in reporting, in order to support the financial sector to improve transparency and drive better practice.

Source: walkfree.org/projects/strengthening-supply-chain-transparency

How to use this page

This page is divided into three sections:

  1. Minimum Requirements – where you can explore the data regarding the elements of minimum compliance under the Act, such a statement being approved by the Board.
  2. Home Office Guidance – where you can explore metrics related to addressing modern slavery in supply chains, which stem from official guidance. Examples include whether companies disclose their supply chains, conduct risk assessment, or have whistleblowing mechanisms.
  3. Investment Portfolios – where you can explore sector-specific metrics developed in consultation with industry experts to understand modern slavery risks for asset managers, such as having a human rights policy for investments or engaging with investee companies.

Each graph can be filtered by Year (Year of statement publication), AUM category, Geography, and membership including FTSE 100 and PRI.

Under Section 54 of the 2015 Modern Slavery Act (MSA), the UK government requires all companies with a turnover of over £36 million per annum to release an annual statement on the actions they are taking to tackle modern slavery in their direct operations and supply chains.

Our findings show that of the 91 asset managers identified as within scope of the MSA, 87 per cent (79) had a modern slavery statement. We also found that asset managers are falling short on compliance with the minimum requirements under the MSA, which require each statement must be signed by a director (or equivalent), approved by the Board of Directors (or equivalent management body), and linked from the company’s homepage.

Beyond minimum requirements in the MSA, the accompanying Home Office statutory guidance outlines the actions companies should take to ensure their supply chains are free from exploitation. From this guidance, we developed further 14 metrics.

Our research highlights several concerning findings. Of the 79 statements assessed:

  • 28 per cent failed to disclose they have policies to address modern slavery.
  • 30 per cent did not disclose whether they facilitate any training on this topic.
  • More than 50 per cent did not disclose any due diligence to address the risks of modern slavery in their supply chains.

Disclosure Rates by Asset Manager

Answered YES to the core metrics (out of 11 metrics)

Investors which do not integrate ESG considerations into their investment practices and decision making are failing in their fiduciary duties. Modern slavery is clearly a human rights and labour standards issue and should be considered as part of the S in ESG. Reporting on addressing modern slavery risks in MSA statements is good practice, offering an opportunity for asset managers to show transparency and leadership.

Despite this, most asset managers did not disclose modern slavery risks as a consideration in their investment decisions.

  • 44% disclosed conducting some form of due diligence on human rights or modern slavery in their portfolios.
  • Only 13% disclosed engaging directly with investee companies to address modern slavery through social audits, self-assessment reviews, filing shareholder resolutions, or training.
